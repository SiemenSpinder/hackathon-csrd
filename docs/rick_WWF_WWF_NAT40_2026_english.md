# EDITORIAL

![img-2.jpeg](img-2.jpeg)

The accelerated degradation of nature, just like climate change, constitutes a systemic threat to our societies and economies. The collapse of biodiversity, air, soil, and water pollution, the scarcity of natural resources, and the degradation of ecosystems directly threaten the stability of our societies and the resilience of economic models.

In the face of this reality, the responsibility of companies is decisive.

Yet, while nature is gradually finding its place in discourse, issues such as pollution, harm to species and ecosystems, or pressure on resources like water, remain largely underestimated. They continue to be marginalized in strategic decisions, and corporate reporting still lags significantly behind progress made on climate. This asymmetry is concerning, as climate and nature are inseparable: they form the two sides of the same ecological crisis and call for ambitious, science-based responses. The entry into force of the CSRD directive and the European ESRS standards, particularly those dedicated to nature topics (E2 to E5), marked a major political advance. It sent a clear signal: transparency on impacts, risks, and dependencies related to nature is no longer optional. However, reporting must not become an end in itself. It should serve as a lever to redirect decisions, invest differently, and align economic models with planetary boundaries.

In this context, WWF France is publishing this first assessment report on the consideration of "nature" issues in the sustainability reports of CAC 40 companies. By analyzing key themes—pollution, water, biodiversity, circular economy—this report highlights encouraging initiatives but also a persistent gap between discourse and actions or the resources actually mobilized. It underscores the need to move from a logic of compliance and communication to robust "nature" transition plans, grounded in science, integrated into investment decisions, and accompanied by credible monitoring mechanisms.

This report is above all a call to action. At a time when companies play a decisive role in the collective ecological trajectory, it is time to fully assume their central position and their capacity to guide the transformation of the economy. Integrating nature at the heart of strategies and investments means recognizing that it is not a peripheral issue but a pillar of economic resilience, innovation, and social justice.

WWF will continue to uphold this requirement, alongside economic and institutional actors, to ensure that the ecological transition is not merely declared but truly financed, implemented, and measurable.

Alexandra Palt, President of WWF France

# CONTEXT AND ISSUES

Nature-related issues are taking an increasingly prominent place in corporate strategy, as awareness grows of their interdependencies with ecosystems and the vital role of nature for the economy. The combination of these interdependencies with planetary boundaries now makes nature a highly strategic topic for economic actors. This dynamic has significantly accelerated since 2020, particularly due to the COP15¹, the entry into force of the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD)², and more recently with the publication of the IPBES report on businesses and biodiversity (IPBES, 2026).

In this context, the CSRD introduces the concept of a transition plan, a fundamental tool to support corporate adaptation. Similar to what has been observed with climate, the gradual emergence of nature transition plans (NTPs) is underway, expected to become widespread quickly. Several expert organizations—including WWF, CDP, and more recently the Taskforce on Nature-related Financial Disclosures (TNFD)³—have contributed to structuring the reference framework, converging towards a consensus on five key components necessary for transformative action: foundations (notably double materiality analyses), metrics and targets, implementation strategies, stakeholder engagement strategies, and governance. These components are also recognized as central by the latest IPBES report (IPBES, 2026).

# OBJECTIVE AND METHODOLOGY OF THE STUDY

The main objective of this study is to assess the level of structuring and maturity of the integration of nature-related issues within major French companies.

The analysis focuses on CAC 40 companies, chosen for their representativeness, the index's notoriety, and the possibility of comparison over time and ranking. It relies exclusively on companies' Universal Registration Documents (URD), to ensure the comparability of the analyzed information, its audited nature, and its growing role as a reference document for stakeholders.

The study concentrates on the information disclosed within the "nature" sections of the CSRD, namely the European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS)⁴ E2 (pollution), E3 (water and oceans), E4 (biodiversity), and E5 (resources and circular economy), analyzing for each the companies' practices on each of the key components of NTPs (without directly measuring the absolute impacts of companies on nature). It should be noted that similar work has been carried out on companies' climate transition plans (WWF, 2025a).

Three relatively homogeneous groups can be identified:

A leading group of nine companies with scores ranging from 52 to 40/100, primarily consisting of businesses with agricultural upstream operations, thus with high impacts and strong dependencies on nature. This group is characterized by a highly insufficient understanding of their nature-related issues, with entire themes sometimes excluded following materiality analyses (e.g., systemic banks unanimously deeming all "nature" issues as non-material), and deep gaps in terms of commitments and practices. This finding is alarming as many of these actors, despite often indirect impacts, bear considerable responsibility for ecosystem degradation (particularly financial players and the communication sector).

# KEY INSIGHTS BY TRANSITION PLAN COMPONENT

## FOUNDATIONS

The fundamental question of materiality analyses is still largely poorly mastered, with 57% of companies presenting analysis results that are misaligned with WWF’s assessment.

## METRICS & TARGETS

The upstream part of value chains is largely neglected, with a vast majority of companies excluding it from the scope of tracked metrics and established targets. Furthermore, while 80% of companies have SBTi-validated targets, only one has adopted SBTN-aligned targets to date.

## IMPLEMENTATION STRATEGY

The action plans of CAC 40 companies are often poorly structured, insufficiently deployed across the entire value chain, and very rarely accompanied by financing plans.

## ENGAGEMENT STRATEGY

Stakeholder engagement remains largely declarative (75%), with only 17% of companies having a structured and coherent approach.

## GOVERNANCE

Governance is the most mature component in the analyzed reports. However, nature-related issues often remain diluted within broader CSR approaches. Notably, 68% of companies do not include nature-related indicators in the variable compensation of their executives.

# STUDY CONTEXT AND OBJECTIVES

Since the early 2000s, nature-related issues have gradually taken on strategic importance in businesses. Long addressed from the angle of compliance, communication, or risk management, they are now recognized as key factors for sustainable economic performance, value chain resilience, and long-term competitiveness. This shift occurs in a context of rapid biodiversity erosion and increasing pressure on natural resources, now clearly identified as systemic risks to the global economy (IPBES, 2026).

In recent years, this momentum has accelerated with the adoption of the Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework and the entry into force of the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD). The latter marks a structuring step by establishing a harmonized sustainability reporting framework based on common standards (ESRS), a double materiality analysis, and a strategic approach to environmental performance. Beyond transparency, the CSRD aims to transform practices by encouraging companies to structure their objectives, trajectories, and action plans while strengthening coherence between strategy, governance, and operational management.

In this context, the CSRD enshrines a key concept: that of the transition plan. Already widely used for climate issues, the transition plan aims to coherently articulate a company’s strategy, its environmental objectives, operational levers, and investments in a measurable transformation perspective. Applied to nature-related issues, the nature transition plan (NTP) can be defined as a structured framework describing how a company intends to reduce its impacts, strengthen the resilience of its activities, and contribute to the preservation and restoration of ecosystems through clear objectives, credible trajectories, and operational actions monitored over time.

The principle of the nature transition plan is also closely linked to the central messages and concepts of the IPBES report dedicated to businesses (IPBES, 2026): the need for transformative changes to halt and reverse biodiversity loss, the definition of clear transformation pathways, and the integration of biodiversity into strategies, business models, and investment decisions.

While the formalization of transition plans is now relatively advanced for climate issues, it remains emerging for nature-related issues, notably due to the lack of stabilized methodological frameworks. In response, WWF has been heavily involved since 2024 in supporting companies in structuring their nature transition plans through publications (WWF, 2024a; WWF 2024b; WWF, 2024c; WWF, 2025a; WWF, 2025b; WWF, 2025c), events, and operational partnerships aimed at accelerating the adoption of these approaches and promoting trajectories aligned with science and global nature protection goals.

In this context, this report has three main objectives:

- Assess the maturity level of French companies regarding nature transition plans, both to document the current state of practices (identify gaps and best practices) and to encourage their structuring and dissemination;
- Disseminate this comparative and readable analysis framework, built using the best available tools (Science Based Targets Network, Taskforce on Nature-related Financial Disclosures, etc.), by aligning it with reflections already carried out by other leading actors and initiatives (World Benchmarking Alliance, Carbon Disclosure Project, ACT Biodiversity, see Appendix p. 122);
- Formulate recommendations for companies, public authorities, and other stakeholders to strengthen the adoption and use of reference resources by companies, improve the institutional framework in which they operate, and thus enable them to rapidly build and follow transition trajectories aligned with science and international objectives.

To achieve this, our analysis focuses on an emblematic sample: CAC 40 companies, through the examination of their CSRD publications and universal registration documents, particularly with regard to ESRS standards related to nature (ESRS E2 to E5: pollution, water and oceans, biodiversity and ecosystems, resources and circular economy).

These evaluations and monitoring of corporate practices in nature preservation are set to be expanded and strengthened, particularly through our collaborations with organizations such as the World Benchmarking Alliance and the Carbon Disclosure Project.

Finally, note that the results of this report will contribute to one of the major objectives of the National Biodiversity Strategy 20:30, namely monitoring the "number of companies having published a 'biodiversity' transition plan [...] to encourage momentum and achieve a doubling of the number of published plans by 20:30" (DGALN, 2023).

This assessment focuses on disclosures related to "nature" issues found in the 2024 Universal Registration Documents (URDs) of CAC 40 companies, in the context of the first year of CSRD implementation. This scope intentionally excludes climate issues (ESRS E1), already covered by a separate analysis (see WWF, 2025a).

The assessment thus covers standards ESRS E2 to E5 (pollution, freshwater and marine resources, biodiversity and ecosystems, resources and circular economy), focusing on the cross-cutting structural elements that constitute nature transition plans (NTPs): double materiality analysis, metrics and targets, implementation strategy, stakeholder engagement strategy, and nature governance.

The analysis is based exclusively on information published in the URDs, without using external sources or interpreting beyond the elements explicitly documented by the companies. This approach provides a structured basis for our analysis (theoretical presence of all NTP components within the ESRS), comparability of information (identical documentary basis for all companies), and the accuracy of information (audited data). It should also encourage companies to include in their future URDs—if not already the case—all relevant elements they possess in relation to NTPs.

# ANALYSIS STRUCTURE

The evaluation of CSRD reports was structured around five key components*, commonly used in methodological frameworks for climate and nature transition plans (see TPT, 2023; TNFD, 2025; etc.), as represented in Figure 1: foundations, metrics and targets, implementation strategy, engagement strategy, governance.

![img-7.jpeg](img-7.jpeg)
Figure 1: Structure of the components of a nature transition plan

The analysis of these components was first conducted by exploring the information provided in the "biodiversity transition plans" section of the URDs (included in ESRS E4). However, since this section is optional and, in practice, rarely completed, information corresponding to NTP components could be found in other sections of the generic ESRS and nature ESRS (double materiality analyses, metrics, targets, action plans, governance, etc.).

We nevertheless encourage companies to use the E4 standard—section 4.1 "Biodiversity and ecosystems transition plan"—as the foundation for disclosing their nature transition plan in the future, by including the various environmental domains in an ecosystemic vision.

The analysis of these components within the URDs led to the scoring of each, then to the sum of these scores to obtain a final score out of 100. To assign relative importance to these components, a weighting was established, with the aim of prioritizing the coherence of companies' practices and operational practices. While this weighting is generally balanced, greater importance was indeed given to implementation strategies (including action plans and financing plans), in order to give a predominant place to operational practices and their links with other key components (see Figure 2).

# ASSESSMENT AND SCORING OF MATURITY LEVELS

The scoring exercise primarily aims to determine whether the analyzed companies are merely complying with regulatory requirements, whether they are following a coherent transition pathway (i.e., intermediate), or whether they demonstrate a credible level of commitment and practices (i.e., advanced).

Thus, for each of the PTN sub-components characterized in the analysis grid (see Table 2), an assessment was conducted using a four-level maturity scale:

| 0 - NON-ALIGNED | The company produces disclosures or non-disclosures that are problematic from WWF's perspective |
| --- | --- |
| 1 - COMPLIANT | The company meets the minimum transparency requirements of the CSRD and associated ESRS |
| 2 - COHERENT | The company goes beyond the minimum compliance required by the CSRD by integrating some of the key expectations of EFRAG¹ (pre-Omnibus) and WWF (see "expected elements" in Table 2) |
| 3 - CREDIBLE | The company achieves an advanced maturity level, with a robust approach aligned with the best methodological frameworks and best practices (i.e., aligned with the "expected elements" in Table 2) |

¹ EFRAG (European Financial Reporting Advisory Group) is a European body established in 2001 that advises the European Commission on accounting standards and, since 2022, on the development of sustainability reporting standards (ESRS).

# METHODOLOGICAL LIMITATIONS

## Limitation related to the sector-agnostic approach of the analysis grid

The PTN analysis method is based on the principle of equivalent treatment of CAC 40 companies, regardless of their sector of activity. This methodological choice implies, for example, that industrial companies and service companies are analyzed using the same criteria and with the same level of requirement, even though the materiality of their nature-related issues could be considered dissimilar, as could the need for transformation of their activities.

This approach does not aim to deny these sectoral differences but to ensure a cross-cutting and comparable reading of corporate strategies, while reaffirming that all companies — as rightly noted in the recent IPBES report (IPBES, 2026) — generate impacts, have dependencies, and face environmental risks and opportunities, regardless of their sector.

Furthermore, in all company factsheets (see "Company factsheets" section, p. 41), a reminder of the sectoral context and its issues has been added to contextualize the assessment.

## Choice of a limited documentary scope

The deliberate choice to base analyses exclusively on the 2024 URD of companies ensures the comparability of the information processed but also entails a number of limitations inherent to the nature of the documents analyzed (potentially non-exhaustive), the evolving regulatory framework, and the choices made in constructing the analysis grid. This approach primarily aims to ensure equal treatment of the evaluated companies and to avoid any bias related to differentiated access to information outside the URD. It should be noted that the URD constitutes a particularly relevant entry point, as a public, regulated, audited document, and built on the basis of the CSRD, thus providing a structuring perspective for environmental strategies.

## Limitation related to the absence of assessment of absolute impacts on nature

This study evaluates the structuring of strategies and practices, and the reporting of companies, without measuring their actual impacts on nature. Two companies with similar scores may thus have very different concrete impacts on ecosystems. The analysis therefore primarily reflects the maturity of strategies and the quality of disclosures.

## Evolution of the European regulatory framework

As the discussions currently stand, the directions taken by the Omnibus directives, particularly targeting the CSRD, reflect more of a deregulation logic than genuine simplifications, and align with a dynamic that is out of step with the environmental imperatives that companies must address.

Indeed, whether through the reduction in the scope of companies required to publish ESG information (over 90% of companies excluded in Europe and internationally) or via the new rules on value chains that limit transparency requirements to companies outside the CSRD's scope, the new version of the CSRD misses the opportunity to effectively structure and support companies on these strategic issues.

The initial version of the regulatory framework, though imperfect, provided a relevant structure for companies' environmental commitments, not only from a disclosure perspective but also in terms of strategic development.

This is why our analysis was based on these initial standards, in line with the reporting practices adopted by first-wave CSRD companies during their first reporting cycle.

Given that the NAT 40 index is intended to be produced periodically, the authors reserve the right to evolve the associated analysis grid, with the constraint of having to reconcile future normative developments with the comparability of results over time.

# ANALYSIS OF RESULTS

The overall assessments included in the NAT 40 index reveal that companies generally have highly insufficient practices across all nature-related themes and key components of transition plans. The relatively heterogeneous scores nonetheless show a variety of maturity trajectories, with some good practices that point the way forward.

# FOUNDATIONS

Key trend:

More than one in two CAC 40 companies (57%, or 23 companies) present a materiality analysis that is misaligned with WWF's assessment⁸.

Even when double materiality analyses are deemed compliant with the regulatory framework, based on auditors' conclusions, we observe significant heterogeneity in the quality and depth of the methodological approaches adopted by companies. Several factors explain these discrepancies.

Firstly, no standardized methodological framework is currently imposed on companies for conducting their double materiality analysis. While the CSRD and the EFRAG's environmental standards (ESRS) provide a common foundation, based on shared language and an intent to link impact materiality with financial materiality, it ultimately falls to each company to interpret the text, apply the requirements, and determine the criticality of its material issues. This interpretative leeway mechanically results in highly variable levels of precision and transparency from one company to another.

These discrepancies are particularly pronounced when the analysis covers the value chain beyond the scope of direct operations. Generally, few companies document their impacts, dependencies, and risks beyond their Tier 1 suppliers, often with limited granularity, relying on broad information scales (e.g., national or regional), which are insufficient to finely characterize the actual pressures on nature.

# BIODIVERSITY MATERIALITY: A GAP BETWEEN DISCOURSE AND SECTORAL REALITIES

By way of illustration, companies such as Thales, STMicroelectronics, and Sistrus do not consider biodiversity a material issue in their reporting. Yet these three companies operate in sectors heavily dependent on raw materials with significant impacts on biodiversity, whether through:

- The extraction and sourcing of mineral and metal resources (rare earths, critical metals);
- The manufacturing of electronic components and finished products (aerospace and military equipment) involving resource-, energy-, and water-intensive processes based on complex and dispersed supply chains.

In this context, it is problematic that companies operating in sectors so closely linked to ecosystems do not transparently disclose information about their upstream value chain and their interdependence with biodiversity.

## **FINANCIAL INSTITUTIONS: FINANCING WITHOUT ASSOCIATED IMPACTS**

## **METRICS & TARGETS**

### **Key Trend:**

CAC 40 companies largely tend to neglect the upstream part of their value chain, both in metric tracking and target setting.

A significant portion of CAC 40 companies currently present relatively developed metric coverage for tracking their direct operations, which remain the most advanced scope in non-financial reporting. However, this coverage remains largely insufficient when it comes to robustly addressing impacts and dependencies across the entire value chain, particularly upstream and, to a lesser extent, downstream.

When CAC 40 companies identify an issue as material, they primarily focus their measurement efforts on their own activities. This translates into tracking water withdrawal and discharge volumes, assessing discharge quality, measuring water consumption by activity, identifying sites located near key biodiversity areas, or deploying circular economy initiatives (such as closed-loop systems).

While these practices represent relative progress within the scope of direct operations, they remain unevenly deployed across the panel of companies and primarily reflect the performance of a limited number of firms. The highest-ranked companies stand out through broader and more structured metric coverage, particularly on freshwater and circular economy issues, whereas most players remain confined to a minimal set of indicators, merely for compliance purposes.

Thus, while the metrics required by CSRD standards are generally respected for direct operations—and sometimes supplemented by pre-existing indicators adapted to sector-specific features—this dynamic should not obscure persistent gaps in systemic vision, particularly regarding indirect impacts and upstream dependencies, which nonetheless constitute major risk areas.

The lack of supply chain traceability is one of the main blind spots in CAC 40 disclosures. Some companies, particularly those whose value chains heavily rely on agricultural raw materials, use certification systems to compensate for the opacity of information related to products placed on the market. However, while these mechanisms represent a first step, they cannot substitute for robust and granular information flows enabling the tracing of impacts and dependencies down to the production plot.

For the industrial sector at large, the lack of information traceability is even more pronounced. Many companies explain this situation by the volatility of raw material markets, which limits their ability to stabilize their supply chains and, consequently, to gather precise location data on material origins. They frequently resort to proxies and external assessment services (e.g., Ecovadis ratings) to characterize associated impacts.

While market volatility does indeed pose operational constraints, it cannot alone justify the absence of traceability. Solutions exist, such as developing longer-term supply relationships, establishing sectoral coalitions, or sharing data among players in the same value chain, which can secure flows while improving knowledge of environmental impacts. However, these levers remain marginally used, limiting both the quality of disclosures and companies' ability to define relevant and localized mitigation pathways.

# **THE COMPLEXITY OF MANAGING AGRICULTURAL TRANSITION WITHOUT FINE VISIBILITY ON THE UPSTREAM**

The top 5 of the ranking includes companies whose business models largely depend on a significant agricultural upstream: Kering, LVMH, Carrefour, L'Oréal, Hermès.

While these companies occupy the top positions in the overall ranking, none of them reach the average score on metrics and targets chapters. This situation is largely explained by a lack of information and precision in disclosures related to the upstream part of the value chain, despite it being identified as a major lever for impact and dependency.

Due to the absence of fine and localized traceability of their agricultural supplies, many companies rely on so-called "sustainable" certifications as a substitute solution, though these alone do not enable effective transition management at the territorial level. Among the frequently used mechanisms are:

- RSPO for palm oil,
- Rainforest Alliance or Fairtrade for certain agricultural materials,
- Labels or sector-specific standards for certain value chains (e.g., cocoa, cotton, coffee).

While these tools represent useful first steps, they cannot be considered sufficient given current environmental challenges. They are often based on compliance and means-based logics, without enabling robust information flow down to the production plot or precise assessment of the state of the ecosystems concerned.

On the freshwater theme, some companies nevertheless stand out for more advanced practices. This is notably the case for Hermès, which deploys an extensive range of pressure and nature state indicators, in line with SBTN methodology principles, including: total water consumption, water consumption by process type (tanneries, textiles, metallurgy, livestock farming), water intensity indicators (consumption per production unit), tracking of withdrawals and water sources (surface water, boreholes, municipal water). The share of reused or recycled water, volumes of treated water reinjected into industrial processes or reused for irrigation, as well as water quality monitoring, supplemented by nature state indicators.

Nevertheless, despite the existence of structured environmental strategies, a persistent gap remains between these approaches and the real robustness of these companies' business models in the face of upcoming environmental transformations. Risks related to supply disruptions, increased raw material price volatility, agricultural yield degradation, or freshwater resource scarcity highlight a common vulnerability: the lack of effective control and strengthened cooperation with actors upstream in the value chain.

**Key Trend:**

80% of CAC 40 companies (32 out of 40) have adopted science-based climate targets through the SBTi framework, while only one CAC 40 company has so far published science-based targets for nature via the SBTN framework.

The CAC 40 highlights a marked imbalance between the treatment of climate issues and those related to nature. While 80% of companies have adopted science-based climate targets through the SBTi framework (32 out of 40), only one company has, to date, disclosed science-based targets for nature via the SBTN framework. This gap is partly explained by a more stabilized and widely adopted normative framework on climate, now integrated into reporting practices within the CSRD context. Conversely, nature-related frameworks, although now available, remain underutilized, reflecting the difficulty for companies to translate complex and localized ecological issues into quantified trajectories.

Some companies, without yet mobilizing formalized scientific frameworks, nevertheless define so-called contextual targets. These approaches constitute a relevant first level of ambition, insofar as they are based on a territorialized understanding of issues, relying on the local context, the guidance of competent authorities, and, more rarely, the contributions of local populations.

While these approaches do not yet ensure full alignment with reference scientific frameworks, they reflect a willingness to contextualize actions and can serve as an intermediate step toward more robust targets. They thus fit into a progression logic, moving from qualitative (predominant in CAC 40 disclosures) or contextual targets toward fully science-based trajectories.

# TOWARD THE ADOPTION OF SCIENCE-BASED TARGETS FOR NATURE-RELATED ISSUES

Some CAC 40 companies are beginning to structure targets aligned with the Science Based Targets for Nature (SBTN) framework, illustrating a gradual increase in scientific approaches to nature-related issues.

Kering stands out as a pioneer by having deployed several SBTN targets, including:

- A strengthened commitment to zero deforestation and zero conversion of natural ecosystems, supported by more detailed analyses of land-use changes in its leather supply chains;
- A target to reduce its land footprint by 3% by 2050, exceeding the minimum requirements of the SBTN framework, based on increased use of recycled materials and those from regenerative agriculture, as well as the development of circular models;
- Landscape engagement targets aimed at improving ecological conditions in key sourcing areas, through projects validated by SBTN.

Other companies, such as Unibail-Rodamco-Westfield™ or Hermès, have so far defined contextual targets, particularly in water-stressed areas, which constitute an intermediate step before the announced deployment of SBTN targets in the short term.

# IMPLEMENTATION STRATEGY

The formalization and implementation of action plans on nature-related issues vary widely among CAC 40 companies. Among the most advanced players, action plans are relatively developed in terms of the types of actions and issues covered, particularly in their direct operations. We note, in particular, a good practice among some companies of linking internal policies on material issues to sets of actions that credibly address an established target.

This structuring between policy, action, and target should therefore be considered an objective for developing a credible nature transition strategy across all material issues.

Indeed, for the vast majority of companies, action plans are not (or very little) structured, with certain actions only partially addressing a given impact, even though it is identified across the company's direct operations. This may, for example, correspond to the development of a pilot action to reduce water withdrawals at a limited number of sites, without information on the scaling-up of such a measure.

Furthermore, we identify a real lack of transparency regarding the quantified results of actions implemented by companies, which are often presented through a qualitative narrative without associated metrics.

In direct operations, responses, when provided, are too often disparate—that is, without clear explanations of the prioritization of the developed action plan and without consideration of all issues identified as material. On the topic of pollution, for example, some companies mainly target atmospheric pollutants in their action plans, without developing the same actions on microplastics or concerning substances, even though these are considered material due to their polluting impacts on water and soil. This lack of detail is even more pronounced

in the area of biodiversity, which appears to be the least well-covered issue in terms of pressure reduction actions, as it is too often approached from the angle of species (e.g., proximity to sensitive areas), thus neglecting land-use change or the dependence of activities on various ecosystem services directly linked to the value chain.

Indeed, actions remain clearly insufficient when it comes to addressing impacts and dependencies across the entire value chain, particularly upstream. Companies minimize the impact of their various suppliers and logistics partners in their action plans (particularly in terms of pollution, water withdrawals, and land artificialization), not adopting the same standards for integrating "nature" issues as in their direct operations, and thus largely considering themselves not responsible for their practices¹⁶. As a result, many actions developed toward this upstream chain are limited to risk management or compliance (e.g., external ESG assessments, integration of qualitative criteria or ESG principles into procurement policies, etc.), without a credible plan to reduce impacts.

Entire segments of upstream chains are thus overlooked (e.g., extraction of critical minerals and metals, production of processed materials subsequently used in direct operations), limiting the overall credibility of companies' action plans. The geographic fragmentation of value chains and the complexity due to the number of intermediaries lead to ignoring or neglecting a large part of these companies' impacts, and to considering cost as the sole compass for procurement objectives and strategy.

Finally, regarding the downstream part of the value chain, few practices are developed by companies to question and transform the use and consumption of the goods and services produced. The linear production system¹⁷ remains the dominant norm, despite some interesting examples of repair or refurbishment of sold products.

# TOWARD RESPONSIBLE WATER MANAGEMENT ACROSS THE ENTIRE VALUE CHAIN

The Kering Group, whose activities have a significant impact on freshwater withdrawals and consumption, has implemented a robust action plan covering its upstream operations and direct activities, notably through the use of water-efficient equipment and dedicated technologies aimed at reducing its footprint.

By combining this approach with a transition to regenerative agriculture and the definition of precise standards at each stage of its water-related value chain, such as in the Arno basin where a science-based water withdrawal reduction target has been set, Kering is structuring an interesting action plan that should now be deployed across all watersheds and priority sites.

The impacts of several companies can have cumulative effects, potentially crossing ecological tipping points, leading to a potentially irreversible loss of biodiversity with considerable economic, social, and ecological consequences (well-established fact).

Nearly two out of three CAC 40 companies (65%, or 26 companies) have not presented any financial metrics related to nature themes.

# STAKEHOLDER ENGAGEMENT STRATEGY

## Key Trend:

While stakeholder engagement is widely formalized within the CAC 40, it remains largely declarative—75% of companies stop at the minimum compliance requirements, with only about 17% having a structured and coherent approach.

This lack of a structured stakeholder engagement approach is directly reflected in the quality of value chain mappings produced by companies. The majority of them remain at a low level of maturity (basic compliance), primarily involving the mapping of stakeholders across certain parts of the value chain. These mappings are, in most cases, insufficiently precise, both geographically and functionally, limiting the ability to robustly identify impacts, dependencies, risks, and opportunities associated with upstream and downstream activities. While this level serves as a necessary foundation, it remains largely inadequate to address operational challenges.

This weakness in mapping fuels a recurring discourse of under-consultation of the extended value chain (cf. Key Trend "Metrics and Targets" p. 23), particularly when it comes to documenting dependencies on natural resources, pressures on ecosystems, or those associated with suppliers and end uses. Without a detailed and shared vision of the value chain, engagement efforts struggle to move beyond a formal level, lacking clear operational impact.

## GOVERNANCE

CAC 40 companies provide an overview of how sustainability issues are integrated into their strategic decision-making, risk management systems, and other governance processes.

Governance is thus the area where companies' maturity, on average, is most advanced, particularly due to alignment with the principles of integrating sustainability themes defined in France (e.g., AFEP-MEDEF code¹).

Indeed, in the majority of CSRD reports, companies mention having established structured sustainability governance at multiple levels, typically with a

strategic decision-making duo at the board of directors and executive committee levels. The former is often supported by one or more committees including sustainability, audit, and risk prerogatives. The latter generally has a "sustainability" officer to bridge environmental and economic strategies.

It is also common to find detailed information on working groups or dedicated teams for the operational implementation of sustainability issues, with structures that are more or less structured and centralized.

Nevertheless, "nature" issues most often remain diluted within generic frameworks (e.g., "environment," "sustainability," "ESG"), which limits the readability and credibility of the governance mechanisms presented¹. The vast majority of companies do not clearly explain how material nature issues are taken into account, with approaches often partial, focused on certain themes like circularity, and lacking transparency on the organization of roles and responsibilities internally. This environmental approach is also still largely structured around climate, which hinders the operational rollout of strategic orientations across different business lines, including within companies that have reached an intermediate level of maturity.

Another governance topic still under development in most companies is staff training.

Many CAC 40 companies have not defined training programs specifically dedicated to material nature issues and remain primarily at the stage of general awareness, across all hierarchical levels, and only on certain key issues (e.g., circularity or biodiversity). Even among the most advanced companies, these trainings often target specific themes and teams, such as procurement on deforestation or operational site management on water management. This assessment highlights still limited maturity and a lack of preparation within organizations for upcoming transformations in their business lines and the new expertise required, both to reduce the impacts of their activities and to develop new business opportunities.

## TRAINING AS A STRUCTURING LEVER FOR ECOLOGICAL TRANSFORMATION

The Veolia Group has training programs covering several dimensions of nature related to its business lines (water, pollution, waste management) and has initiated dedicated biodiversity training for its teams, with the overall goal of reaching an average of 30 hours of training per employee by 2027 (a target already exceeded in 2024). Veolia has also co-developed "Terra Academia," an external institution aimed at accelerating training development for jobs related to ecological transformation.

A next step would be to further strengthen training efforts by 2027 to more clearly demonstrate how all these mechanisms contribute to improving environmental services and reducing the negative impacts of the group's activities.

## Key Trend:

Nearly two out of three CAC 40 companies (68%, or 27 companies) do not have nature KPIs in the variable compensation of their executives or other employees.

A specific issue within the Governance theme, executive compensation is a revealing indicator of the strategic level of consideration given to a topic by companies.

Similar to the chapter on targets, a marked imbalance appears between the integration of climate issues and nature-related issues in compensation mechanisms: nearly all CAC 40 companies include a climate KPI, compared to a much more limited share for nature themes.

When such nature KPIs exist, they mainly focus either on the implementation of a strategic plan or the development of "business" opportunities (5 companies), or on the reduction of a specific material pressure (8 companies). Quantitatively, the weight of these indicators remains largely undervalued compared to climate (around 5% versus 15%), and generally remains included within a set of "extra-financial" indicators representing an average of 20% of the variable compensation of CAC 40 executives.

# LIMITED ENVIRONMENTAL INCENTIVES IN EXECUTIVE VARIABLE COMPENSATION

L'Oréal has integrated, into the variable compensation of its CEO, the validation of targets from its strategic program "L'Oréal for the Future," three of which are directly related to "nature" themes: water management, respect for biodiversity (via sustainable sourcing), and the preservation of natural resources, particularly in relation to plastic use.

It should nevertheless be noted that these indicators represent less than 10% of the CEO's variable compensation and are not rolled out to all employees benefiting from variable compensation.

# FOCUS ON KEY MESSAGES FROM THE IPBES REPORT ON BUSINESSES AND BIODIVERSITY (IPBES, 2026)

Among the central messages of the latest IPBES report, published in February 2026 and specifically addressing the relationships between businesses and biodiversity, many points corroborate our findings and recommendations.

First and foremost, the report clearly states that all businesses are affected by biodiversity, either directly or indirectly, due to their dependencies on ecosystems and their impacts along value chains. It highlights that these impacts are often indirect, making improved traceability of supply chains essential.

From this perspective, the report also emphasizes the need for businesses to conduct robust materiality analyses, integrating both their impacts, dependencies, and the risks and opportunities associated with biodiversity. This understanding is presented as the indispensable starting point for defining credible strategies and prioritizing actions.

On the central question of tools available to businesses, IPBES highlights that the tools and methods for measuring impacts and dependencies are now sufficiently developed to guide action. The report emphasizes three fundamental criteria for assessing their relevance: coverage (geographical, sectoral, taxonomic scope, etc.), precision (ability to faithfully represent biodiversity and nature's contributions), and sensitivity, i.e., their capacity to detect changes attributable to business actions, thus underscoring the need to use metrics sensitive to real ecological changes.

The report also stresses the need for businesses to set measurable targets, backed by credible monitoring, and to account for long-term ecological dynamics, including the risks of crossing tipping points that could lead to irreversible biodiversity loss due to the cumulative impacts of economic activities. It highlights that these commitments must translate into operational action plans, at both site and value chain levels, relying notably on a robust action hierarchy and approaches to transforming practices.

Aligning financial flows with biodiversity objectives is another key component identified by the report. It notes that current investments remain largely incompatible with the conservation and restoration of ecosystems and calls for integrating biodiversity into financing, investment, and insurance decisions to support the transition.

The report also underscores the importance of integrating biodiversity into corporate governance, through strategic leadership at the highest level, clear responsibilities, and alignment between strategy, risk management, investment decisions, and business models.

Finally, IPBES concludes that the current conditions in which businesses operate are not compatible with halting and reversing biodiversity loss, and that a transformative change in strategies, business models, and financial systems is necessary.

RECOMMENDATIONS

The analysis of CAC 40 companies' practices allows for the formulation of operational recommendations for public and banking authorities, businesses, financial institutions, auditors, and society with the aim of improving the overall framework of environmental practices.

## 1. RECOMMENDATIONS FOR COMPANIES

### 1.1. Companies must systematically consider their impacts, dependencies, risks, and opportunities (IDRO) related to nature, both for their direct operations and their indirect responsibility scope—thus drastically improving their traceability—and adopt science-based targets, accompanied by operational and funded action plans.

Our analyses highlight that too many companies still limit their materiality analysis to the impacts of their direct activities, ignoring the upstream and/or downstream segments of their value chain due to highly insufficient traceability, and often without considering their dependence on ecosystem services. This must now prompt companies to strengthen their practices and the resources mobilized to meet the materiality challenge, in order to make it the foundation of their transition.

Furthermore, these analyses show that many companies still settle for targets—when they have any—that are not science-based, primarily driven by regulatory compliance or purely endogenous considerations, thus lacking ambition and disconnected from ecosystem realities. Moreover, these targets remain too rarely linked to action plans and the financial resources mobilized. These suboptimal practices do not allow for strategic steering nor a transformation of practices at the scale of the challenges. In particular, no credible transition can be initiated without a clear allocation of resources.

### 1.2 Companies must strengthen their stakeholder engagement practices and formally integrate nature at the heart of their governance systems.

Key stakeholders—such as local authorities, local actors, suppliers, local communities, or civil society—remain largely absent from the co-construction of companies' transition pathways, leading to the neglect of local knowledge and regulations, increased risks of land-use conflicts, and weakened legitimacy, territorial anchoring, and effectiveness of the actions implemented. We recommend that companies adopt collective and holistic approaches in developing their environmental strategies, involving a diversity of stakeholders in a structured, continuous, and decision-making manner.

At the same time, while companies are gradually expanding their governance beyond the sole financial lens—particularly under the influence of integrating climate issues into business models—biodiversity remains largely marginalized. Given that no climate transition plan can be fully effective without a robust and articulated nature transition plan, we urge an urgent catch-up, notably through the integration of nature indicators into executives' variable compensation, the training of decision-makers and employees, and the implementation of dedicated governance processes at all levels of the company (decision-making bodies, strategic departments, operational/business departments).

## 2. RECOMMENDATIONS TO PUBLIC AUTHORITIES

### 2.1 Public authorities should commit to maintaining a robust European legislative framework for nature reporting and make the disclosure of nature transition plans mandatory.

In the context of the simplification planned by the Omnibus directive, we call for preserving the structuring elements of the initial reporting framework defined by the CSRD, to maintain a coherent information structure and the strategic dimension of the exercise. Beyond preserving the fundamental sequence "double materiality analysis – metrics – science-based targets – action plans – financing plans," it is essential to retain, for all nature-related issues, the quantification of financial effects linked to sustainability, the identification of significant impacts at the site level and throughout the value chain, and the full disclosure of this information.

It is also indispensable to retain illustrative and standardized metrics in the CSRD, to concretely support companies by providing operational benchmarks and harmonizing reporting exercises.

Furthermore, to ensure the best consistency across all the aforementioned elements and the greatest effectiveness of companies' nature and climate strategies, we call for making the development of short-term nature transition plans mandatory.

In this regard, WWF recalls that the European Union's competitiveness objective can only be sustainably achieved if companies are able to strengthen the resilience of their business models, a resilience closely linked to that of the ecosystems with which they interact across their expanded scope.

Additionally, actions must be taken to end "harmful subsidies" to biodiversity in France and Europe, replacing them with economic support for the transition of production and consumption methods.

### 2.2 Public authorities should create a centralized portal to facilitate access to public environmental data for companies¹⁰, and enhance existing knowledge on the good ecological status of ecosystems.

WWF recommends strengthening the structuring of public environmental data—particularly concerning the state of ecosystems—currently scattered across multiple organizations (OFB, MNHN, Water Agencies, Ifremer, BRGM, etc.) and platforms (SIE, SIMM, SDAGE, SAGE, Nature France, etc.)¹¹ to simplify their operational use by companies and improve the quality and comparability of information. This centralization would reduce data collection and analysis costs and facilitate the formulation of individual or collective objectives for good ecological status. On this last point, an effective public policy must also consider strengthening public research and expertise on ecological thresholds, particularly for terrestrial ecosystems where knowledge is currently too fragmented.

# 3. RECOMMENDATIONS TO BANKING/ASSURANCE AUTHORITIES

## 3.1 Central banks and prudential authorities must explicitly integrate biodiversity-related risks and dependencies into their supervisory frameworks and macroprudential tools.

The degradation of biodiversity and ecosystems generates both physical and transition financial risks, which can affect the stability of the financial system. To address this, authorities must rely on information from companies' "nature" transition plans and develop science-based specific indicators to assess portfolio exposure and institutions' capacity to manage these risks.

On the financial institutions' side, it is essential to integrate this data into investment, lending, insurance, and underwriting decisions to reduce systemic risk and direct financing toward projects aligned with nature preservation and restoration. Engagement mechanisms and, if necessary, sanctions must be put in place to ensure proper consideration of these risks across all financial activities.

Following the example of the climate indicator developed by the Banque de France based on the ACT methodology, WWF calls for the development of science-based "nature" indicators to assess portfolio exposure to biodiversity-related risks and gradually integrate these elements into supervisory tools, credit ratings, and stress test exercises. The European Central Bank should explore modulating its refinancing conditions and monetary policy operations to incentivize the financing of projects and activities aligned with nature preservation and restoration.

Such an approach would help strengthen the resilience of the financial system, improve capital allocation, and align financial regulation with ecological boundaries.

For more details on our recommendations, refer to WWF, Sustainable Financial Regulations and Central Bank Activities Assessment 2025 (SUSREG 2025).

## 3.2 Financial market authorities (AMF and European equivalents) should develop a framework for assessing the credibility of nature reporting and transition plans, and use it to ensure that companies and financial institutions fully account for biodiversity-related risks and impacts across all their activities.

Financial market authorities protect investors and market stability. Nature-related risks are financially material and can affect the health and resilience of companies. As part of CSRD and ESRX supervision, these authorities must verify that companies publish credible information on nature and, ultimately, nature transition plans, enabling investors and stakeholders to properly assess the level of commitment and preparedness regarding environmental risks and impacts.

The WWF recommends that they rely on recognized methodologies (WWF framework, TNFD, ACT Biodiversity, ATP-Col) to assess these plans, in coordination with other regulators and supervisors to harmonize analysis tools. The results of this assessment should then be used to engage in dialogue with economic actors and, if necessary, to apply sanctions to ensure the robustness of companies and the financial system.

# 4. RECOMMENDATIONS FOR FINANCIAL AND BANKING INSTITUTIONS

## 4.1 Financial institutions must, from their CSRD report published in 2026, consider nature-related issues as material questions and develop a biodiversity strategy aligned with the recommendations of the TNFD and GFANZ.

Financial actors must, urgently, formalize credible materiality analyses covering all their activities, including financing, loans, and all other financial services. These analyses must enable the quantification of the negative impacts of their practices and strengthen existing environmental thematic policies, often weakened by exceptions and omissions¹⁷, to better integrate dependency and value chain issues in order to consider all impacts of financed sectors. At the same time, financial actors must strengthen their commitments to real economy companies so that they set science-aligned targets, develop, and publish robust biodiversity transition plans. This requires the adoption of financing and credit policies conditioned on credible improvements in practices, thus excluding harmful sectoral practices, as well as the modulation of interest rates and returns based on the "nature" impact of financed projects. This also requires deep reflection by the sector on financing conditions, in light of liquidity, returns, and risk issues¹⁸.

## 4.2 Financial institutions must develop innovative financial tools to support an ambitious science-aligned ecological transition.

Financial actors must establish "green" and "brown" classifications covering all "nature" issues and develop financing tools to accelerate the improvement of corporate practices. Numerous opportunities exist to respond to the transformation of economic activities, particularly through the development of dedicated financial mechanisms (green/blue bonds, sustainability-linked loans, biodiversity action or debt funds, shared funds financing conservation activities, nature-based solutions financing, "solutions" company financing, etc.).

At the same time, a gradual divestment from financial service portfolios with proven negative impacts must be conducted. As mentioned in this report, financing transition activities is a key issue, where financial actors play an essential role.

Following the example of what exists for climate¹⁹, financial actors have the capacity to direct financial flows to accompany the progressive transformation of high-impact activities on nature and to support the deployment of sustainable sectors.

Concretely, exposure to high-risk sectors, particularly certain agricultural commodities associated with deforestation or soil degradation, must be rapidly abandoned. Financing must therefore prioritize the transformation of practices, for example through the adoption of agroecological models, strengthening traceability, or providing technical support to producers (see Box - The complexity of managing agricultural transition without fixed upstream visibility p. 24).

# 5. RECOMMENDATIONS FOR AUDITORS

## 5.1 Auditors should explicitly flag materiality gaps and stakeholder blind spots

When auditors identify gaps in materiality analysis, inconsistencies, or omissions—particularly when these have been raised by key stakeholders (local actors, civil society, communities, scientific experts, or value chain actors)—they should explicitly flag them in their assurance reports on sustainability information.

These findings should notably address the absence or insufficiency of consideration for indirect impacts, ecosystem dependencies, territorialized issues, or significant risks identified upstream by stakeholders.

Such an approach would strengthen transparency, the credibility of "nature" reporting exercises, and the usefulness of audits for public decision-makers, investors, and all users of sustainability information.

## 5.2 Auditors should rely on recognized reference frameworks to assess the credibility of nature transition plans

Auditors should use recognized reference frameworks such as the TNFD, SBTN work, any other relevant scientific framework, as well as the analysis grid proposed in this report, to assess the credibility and internal consistency of "nature" transition plans.

These tools make it possible to evaluate the alignment of strategies, objectives, and action plans with scientific thresholds, sectoral realities, and ESRS requirements, particularly ESRS E4.

The use of shared frameworks would facilitate the identification of elements that could compromise regulatory compliance, as well as the robustness and operationality of nature transition plans, while contributing to the harmonization and strengthening of audit practices.

# EXAMPLE OF A TYPICAL NATURE TRANSITION PLAN

## ADVANCED TRANSFORMATION PRACTICES

This section presents the example of a fictional company with advanced practices for each component of its nature transition plan, with the aim of providing improvement examples for all companies, regardless of their maturity level and sector of activity.

## FOUNDATIONS

The company’s double materiality analysis is based on an in-depth assessment of its impacts, primarily on the upstream side of its high-risk commodities, identified through the High Impact Commodity List (e.g., agricultural: cocoa, soy, cotton; mining: rare earths; metallurgical: steel, aluminum), direct operations, and, secondarily, the downstream side of products throughout their life cycle.

All environmental issues (pollution, water, biodiversity, resources, and circularity) are considered material based on this analysis, conducted according to steps 1 and 2 of the SBTN methodology. This impact analysis is supplemented by an analysis of dependencies (e.g., using the ENCORE tool combined with the TNFD’s sectoral factsheets and the WEF) and risks inspired by the TNFD framework (LEAP approach), particularly to develop a quantitative assessment of activities with negative impacts on nature issues.

To ensure that the company’s activities do not result in impacts on particularly sensitive areas or species, the company uses nature status qualification tools: the WWF’s Risk Filter suite (water and biodiversity) and the IBAT tool (or any local sources associated with fine geolocation) to verify the location of existing sites, projects, and certain strategic suppliers in relation to high ecological stake areas (protected areas or species, high water stress zones, etc.).

All stakeholders are taken into account to significantly strengthen the double materiality analysis, incorporating local data and a focus on the various companies in the value chain. The double materiality analysis is supplemented and validated by an expert committee including external stakeholders (e.g., scientists, Indigenous populations, NGOs, etc.) to ensure the credibility of the approach.

The downstream side is also subject to specific work, in a logic of responsibility for the sustainability, reparability, and end-of-life of goods and services placed on the market. The prioritization of the double materiality analysis is dominated by impact materiality, with financial materiality analyzed secondarily. Additionally, work on a dedicated study of the cost of environmental inaction compared to transition pathways is planned in the short term to inform the Board’s long-term decisions and dialogue with shareholders. Finally, the company publishes a specific chapter presenting a detailed correspondence table between its economic strategy and the objectives derived from global frameworks, such as the Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework.

# METRICS

The management of the company’s nature impacts is primarily based on physical data measured at industrial sites/offices/agricultural areas, with a reinforced prioritization approach. Water indicators (withdrawals, consumption, discharges by use) are monitored for all sites but are subject to enhanced management for a priority scope corresponding to sites identified as closest to high-stake areas, particularly in water stress or ecological sensitivity zones. These sites are subject to monthly monitoring and specific reviews at the company level (e.g., showing the impact of action plans on footprint reduction).

For biodiversity, impact and status indicators are deployed as a priority on this same scope of sensitive sites, based on IBAT data and local ecological studies, allowing the monitoring of habitat evolution or the presence of protected species. Resource flows are tracked in physical volumes, with a focus, for example, on key metals (copper, aluminum, steel), their geographic origin, and their recycling rate. Waste is tracked by category, with a clear distinction between material recovery, energy recovery, and disposal.

Transparent and traceable data, covering both the company’s operations (materials, components, and substances of concern used) and the entire value chain (impacts related to the extraction, processing, and transport of raw materials), make it possible to assess and monitor various local pollution sources, particularly on soils, water, and the atmosphere. This data relies on quantitative metrics broken down by substance categories (including substances of concern and very high concern) and by life cycle stages, to precisely measure contributions to environmental impacts.

A focus is placed on waste management and product end-of-life, including indicators for reduction, transformation, and valorization of material flows. This approach is complemented by phased-out plans for substances of concern and very high concern, accompanied by quantified targets and clearly defined timeframes, aiming, where possible, to anticipate regulatory deadlines.

Similar trajectories are defined for the reduction and elimination of plastics and microplastics, with regular performance monitoring and intermediate milestones to steer the achievement of objectives. When certain upstream data is not yet available at the supplier level, the company uses sectoral and/or geographical proxies, clearly identified as such. A supplier engagement plan prioritizes materials and suppliers contributing most to identified impacts, with an objective of accessing primary data for priority flows by 2027.

This approach allows efforts to be concentrated on high-stakes sites while progressively structuring internal monitoring and management capabilities.

Upstream, the company has also defined SBTN targets linked, for example, to land use, aiming to reduce the land footprint associated with the extraction and transformation of key raw materials. Engagement with suppliers should enable, through contractual performance commitments, the latter to define their own targets on their material issues.

When the level of available data does not yet allow for the deployment of fully scientific targets across the entire value chain, the company relies on intermediate, contextual, or sectoral targets, based on local, regulatory references or in collaboration with other companies in the sector. These targets are intended to be progressively strengthened and converted into scientific targets as upstream coverage and data quality improve.

Upstream, the strategy primarily aims at the progressive transformation of supply chains.

The levers used focus on the durability, repairability, and recyclability of goods, as well as the development of renovation and end-of-life take-back services. This approach helps limit impacts related to final uses without shifting responsibility to customers.

The implementation of this strategy relies on a cross-functional logic, mobilizing all company functions (procurement, engineering, operations, R&D, finance, legal). Nature-related issues are integrated into investment and industrial decision-making processes, with dedicated financial planning identifying "Nature" Capex and Opex to credibly enable the transition of activities and the realization of the action plan (water, circularity, depollution, supply chain transformation). This quantified planning aims to secure the company's long-term trajectory, beyond short-term profitability considerations.

## ENGAGEMENT STRATEGY

The company's engagement strategy is based on a structured mapping of its stakeholders, covering upstream, direct operations, and downstream, with particular attention to affected territories. Upstream and downstream considerations remain very limited, and no analysis is conducted on the maritime impacts of its activities.

Finally, no scientific target-setting methodology (SBTN, contextual targets) is mobilized, and no dedicated financial plan for the nature issue has yet been formalized, despite the mention of alignment with taxonomy for these themes.

## IMPLEMENTATION STRATEGY

### Maturity level: low

TotalEnergies' "nature" strategy focuses primarily on operational sites controlled by the company, through the deployment of environmental management systems, risk and accident prevention guides, particularly on pollution issues. Targeted action plans have been implemented for water management and biodiversity preservation on sensitive sites, with variable indicators allowing for partial assessment of pressure reduction. However, uncontrolled direct operations are not subject to systematic monitoring.

Upstream, the value chain is partially addressed through a responsible purchasing policy that broadly integrates environmental issues, supported by external evaluations by Ecothaïla, performance monitoring, and on-site audits, which may lead to improvement plans.

The absence of dedicated financial planning for "nature" issues constitutes a significant barrier to the credibility and scaling up of the transition plan.

## ENGAGEMENT STRATEGY

### Maturity level: low

TotalEnergies deploys a broad engagement strategy across its various projects, with numerous partnerships and regular dialogue with local stakeholders (such as workers and suppliers) on all operational environmental issues.

Engagement in the upstream value chain is structured through an action and incident monitoring plan, which explicitly includes a framework for the rights of local communities in controlled direct operations (e.g., the implementation of a continuous dialogue complaint mechanism). Nevertheless, the company does not communicate on the content of unresolved complaints or the impacts of this dialogue on the company's local activities. Furthermore, engagement practices remain opaque and thus limited for non-controlled and non-operated projects².

Additionally, the lack of transparency on "nature" advocacy and lobbying, as well as certain existing controversies, reduces the overall credibility of the engagement.

## GOVERNANCE

### Maturity level: low to intermediate

TotalEnergies' governance is structured but remains largely integrated within the environmental/ethics block, without "nature" being truly positioned as a strategic issue. The Board of Directors, through a strategic and CSR committee, the Executive Committee, and business units, demonstrates clear involvement, focused on risk management and operational improvement, also integrated into the company's training programs. However, cross-functional coordination on biodiversity and ecosystems at the Group level is ensured by the HSE department with the support of subsidiaries (less transparency on other issues).

Furthermore, financial incentive mechanisms do not yet integrate "nature" issues for management.

# GLOSSARY

## AR3T
(Avoid, Reduce, Restore, Transform)
Hierarchical action framework used to guide corporate responses to negative impacts on nature. It is based on four complementary levers:
- **Avoid**: prevent negative impacts before they occur;
- **Reduce**: decrease impacts that cannot be avoided;
- **Restore**: restore degraded ecosystems when residual impacts remain;
- **Transform**: implement systemic changes to business models, value chains, or production systems to sustainably reduce pressures on nature.

Source: translated from Science Based Targets Network

## CAC 40
Created in 1987, the CAC 40 is the main stock market index of the Paris financial market. It is a basket composed of 40 shares of French companies. These companies are selected from the 100 French companies with the highest trading volumes.

Source: Ministère de l'Économie et des Finances

## Value Chain
Set of activities necessary for a company to create value, from the extraction of raw materials (upstream) to the final use of products or services and their end-of-life (downstream). The value chain includes suppliers, subcontractors, partners, distributors, customers, and other stakeholders, including those outside the company's direct operational scope.

Source: Directive (EU) 2022/2464 – CSRD;
EFRAG, ESRS 1 – General Requirements, 2023.

## Cost of Inaction
Assessment of the economic, financial, social, and environmental costs associated with the absence or insufficiency of actions to prevent or reduce negative impacts on nature and climate. It notably includes risks related to ecosystem degradation, loss of ecosystem services, supply chain disruptions, price volatility, and regulatory or reputational impacts.

Source: TEEB, The Economics of Ecosystems and Biodiversity – Ecological and Economic Foundations, 2010
Translation: French translation by WWF France from the original English version.

## CSRD
(Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive)
European Directive (EU 2022/2464) establishing a strengthened sustainability reporting framework for companies. It requires the publication of standardized information on environmental, social, and governance (ESG) issues, including across the entire value chain, based on the principle of double materiality.

Source: Directive (EU) 2022/2464
https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/FR/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32022L2464

## Universal Registration Document (URD)
Regulatory document published annually by listed companies, designed to provide comprehensive, consistent, and standardized information on their financial situation, strategy, governance, risk factors, and, where applicable, their extra-financial performance. The URD may integrate sustainability information required by the CSRD, including environmental, social, and governance (ESG) data, and serves as a reference document for investor and public information.

Source: Autorité des marchés financiers (AMF) – Document d'enregistrement universel (URD)

## Double Materiality
Principle according to which a company must assess and disclose:
- its **impact materiality** (the impacts of its activities on the environment and society);
- its **financial materiality** (the risks and opportunities related to sustainability issues that may affect its financial performance).

Source: EFRAG, ESRS 1 – General Requirements, 2023

## ESRS
(European Sustainability Reporting Standards)
European sustainability reporting standards developed by EFRAG, defining disclosure requirements under the CSRD. They cover climate issues, biodiversity, water, pollution, the circular economy, as well as social and governance matters.

Source: European Commission, Delegated Act on ESRS, 2023

GLOSSARY

# State of Nature

Assessment of the condition and health of ecosystems, species, and natural resources, including indicators related to biodiversity, habitat quality, water availability, and ecological functions. The state of nature helps evaluate whether pressures compromise the ability of ecosystems to provide their services.

Source: IPBES, Global Assessment Report on Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services, 2019;
SBTN, Initial Guidance for Business, 2023
Translation: French translation by WWF France from the original English versions.

# Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework (GBF)

Global framework for biodiversity adopted in 2022 at COP15 of the Convention on Biological Diversity. It sets 4 long-term goals (2050) and 23 targets for 2030 aimed at halting biodiversity loss, restoring ecosystems, and redirecting financial flows toward nature-positive activities.

Source: Convention on Biological Diversity,
Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework, 2022
Translation: French translation by WWF France from the original English version.

# IBAT (Integrated Biodiversity Assessment Tool)

Online tool for identifying ecologically sensitive areas (protected areas, key biodiversity habitats, threatened species) to assess potential risks and impacts of economic activities. IBAT is used for planning, risk assessment, and decision-making at the site level.

Source: IBAT Alliance,
IBAT for Business – Guidance and Methodology, 2022
Translation: French translation by WWF France from the original English version.

# Nature Transition Plan (NTP)

A set of objectives, targets, actions, accountability mechanisms, and planned resources aimed at addressing the transition driven by the Global Biodiversity Framework, in which biodiversity loss is halted and reversed by 2030 to put nature on a recovery trajectory by 2050, while respecting planetary boundaries. The plan must describe how the entity will evolve its operations and entire business model to achieve its objectives and align with local, national, and international environmental targets, as well as the best available scientific knowledge on the environment.

Source: WWF, Cataloging change: The urgent need for
Nature Transition Plans, 2024
Translation: French translation by WWF France from the original English version.

# Risk Filter Suite (water and biodiversity)

Set of tools developed by WWF to identify and assess water- and biodiversity-related risks by combining geographic data, environmental pressures, and ecosystem conditions. These tools are used to analyze risks at the site and value chain levels.

Source: WWF, Water Risk Filter;
WWF, Biodiversity Risk Filter
Translation: French translation by WWF France from the original English versions.

# Science-Based Targets for Nature (SBTN)

International methodological framework enabling companies to set science-based targets to reduce their impacts on nature and restore ecosystems. It covers freshwater, land use, biodiversity, and oceans, and is based on an action hierarchy and pathways aligned with planetary boundaries.

Source: Science Based Targets Network,
Initial Guidance for Business, 2023
Translation: French translation by WWF France from the original English version.

# TNFD (Taskforce on Nature-related Financial Disclosures)

International initiative aimed at developing a framework for disclosing nature-related financial risks and opportunities. The TNFD framework is based on the LEAP approach (Locate, Evaluate, Assess, Prepare) to help companies and financial institutions integrate nature-related issues into their strategies and risk management.

Source: TNFD, Recommendations of the Taskforce on
Nature-related Financial Disclosures, Version 1.0, 2023
Translation: French translation by WWF France from the original English version.
